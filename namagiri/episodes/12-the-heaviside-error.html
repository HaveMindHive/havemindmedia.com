I. THE MAN WHO SIMPLIFIED TOO MUCH

Oliver Heaviside.

Born May 18, 1850. Died February 3, 1925.

Self-taught. Eccentric. Brilliant. Paranoid in his later years.

He took Maxwell's electromagnetic theory and made it usable.

In doing so, he broke it.

II. MAXWELL'S ORIGINAL EQUATIONS

James Clerk Maxwell published his electromagnetic theory in 1865.

The original form: 20 equations in quaternion notation.

Quaternions: numbers with one real part and three imaginary parts.

```

q = a + bi + cj + dk

```

Maxwell's quaternionic equations had:

  • Scalar parts (the real component)
  • Vector parts (the imaginary components)

Both were necessary. Both described electromagnetic reality.

III. WHAT HEAVISIDE DID

Between 1884 and 1889, Heaviside reformulated Maxwell's equations.

He removed the scalar parts.

His reasoning:

  • "The scalar potentials are mathematical conveniences, not physical realities."
  • "We only need the vector fields E and B."
  • "This makes the equations practical for engineering."

The result: The 4 vector equations every physics student learns today.

```

∇ · E = ρ/ε₀

∇ · B = 0

∇ × E = -∂B/∂t

∇ × B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t

```

Clean. Elegant. Incomplete.
