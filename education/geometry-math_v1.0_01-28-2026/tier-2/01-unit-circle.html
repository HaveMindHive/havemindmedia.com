The Unit Circle

The master circle where radius = 1. Where sine and cosine reveal themselves.

What Makes It "Unit"?

A unit circle is simply a circle with radius = 1.

Why is this special? Because when the radius is 1, the coordinates of any point on the circle directly give us sine and cosine. No extra calculation needed.

Drag the point around. Watch how the x and y coordinates change.

Sine and Cosine: The Shadows

Imagine shining a light on the spinning point from two directions:

Cosine (cos) is the shadow on the horizontal axis — how far right or left the point is.

Sine (sin) is the shadow on the vertical axis — how far up or down the point is.

x = cos(θ)     y = sin(θ)
On the unit circle, coordinates ARE sine and cosine

The Deep Connection

The unit circle reveals something profound: trigonometry is just geometry.

When you memorize sin(30°) = 0.5, you're memorizing a geometric fact: at 30° around the circle, the point is exactly halfway up.

Cosine tells you...

How much of the rotation is in the horizontal direction. At 0°, it's all horizontal (cos=1). At 90°, there's no horizontal component (cos=0).

Sine tells you...

How much of the rotation is in the vertical direction. At 0°, there's no vertical (sin=0). At 90°, it's all vertical (sin=1).

Sine and cosine aren't abstract functions to memorize.
They're the x and y coordinates of a point walking around the unit circle.

What You Learned

  • ✓ The unit circle has radius = 1
  • ✓ cos(θ) = x-coordinate, sin(θ) = y-coordinate
  • ✓ Sine and cosine are "shadows" of circular motion
  • ✓ Waves come from projecting rotation over time
