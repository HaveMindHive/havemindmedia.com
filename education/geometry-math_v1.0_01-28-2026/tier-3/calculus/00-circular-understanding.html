Circular Understanding

Lesson 0: The Foundation — Velocity is Position Rotated 90°

The Core Principle

On a circle, velocity is always perpendicular to position.

Perpendicular means rotated by 90°.

This single insight unlocks all of calculus.

What This Means

Imagine walking around a circle. At every moment:

  • Position tells you WHERE you are (the arrow from center to you)
  • Velocity tells you WHERE you're GOING (the direction you're walking)

On a circle, these two arrows are always perpendicular — they form a 90° angle. Your direction of motion is always tangent to the circle, which means it's perpendicular to the radius pointing at you.

If position points "outward" from the center, velocity points "sideways" along the circle.

The Mathematical Proof

Let's prove that position and velocity are always perpendicular on the unit circle.

Theorem: Position ⊥ Velocity on the Circle

1
P(θ) = (cos θ, sin θ)
Position on the unit circle at angle θ
2
V(θ) = dP/dθ = (-sin θ, cos θ)
Velocity is the derivative of position
3
P · V = cos θ · (-sin θ) + sin θ · cos θ
Compute the dot product
4
P · V = -cos θ · sin θ + sin θ · cos θ = 0
The terms cancel! Dot product = 0 means perpendicular. ∎

This isn't a coincidence. It's the geometric meaning of the derivative for circular motion: the derivative rotates by 90°.

The Phase Relationship

Look at the velocity components: V = (-sin θ, cos θ). Compare to position: P = (cos θ, sin θ).

Notice that:

  • Velocity X = -sin θ = cos(θ + 90°)
  • Velocity Y = cos θ = sin(θ + 90°)

Velocity is position shifted by 90° in phase.
This is the fundamental relationship of calculus.

The Q-Cycle

If one derivative rotates by 90°, what happens when we keep taking derivatives?

Four derivatives = four 90° rotations = 360° = back to start!

This is the Q-cycle: 1 → i → -1 → -i → 1. It's also why:

  • d/dθ sin(θ) = cos(θ) — one derivative, 90° ahead
  • d²/dθ² sin(θ) = -sin(θ) — two derivatives, 180° = opposite
  • d³/dθ³ sin(θ) = -cos(θ) — three derivatives, 270°
  • d⁴/dθ⁴ sin(θ) = sin(θ) — four derivatives, back to start

Calculus is rotation. Derivatives rotate by +90°. Integrals rotate by -90°.
Everything else is a consequence of this.

Why This Matters

Traditional calculus starts with limits and infinitesimals — useful computational tools, but they obscure the geometric meaning.

When you understand that differentiation = 90° rotation, suddenly:

  • d/dθ sin(θ) = cos(θ) is obvious — cos is sin rotated 90°
  • d/dθ e^(iθ) = i·e^(iθ) is obvious — multiplying by i rotates 90°
  • Integration undoes differentiation is obvious — +90° then -90° = 0°

What You Learned

  • ✓ On a circle, velocity is perpendicular to position
  • ✓ Perpendicular = rotated by 90°
  • ✓ The dot product P · V = 0 proves perpendicularity
  • ✓ Four derivatives = 360° = Q-cycle complete
  • ✓ This is the foundation of all calculus
