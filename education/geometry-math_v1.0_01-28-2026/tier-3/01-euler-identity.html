Euler's Formula: The General Case

The identity is a special case of Euler's formula:

eiθ = cos(θ) + i·sin(θ)
A point at angle θ on the unit circle

This says: when you raise e to an imaginary power iθ, you get a point on the unit circle at angle θ.

The Special Case: θ = π

Now set θ = π (half way around the circle):

Step by Step

1
eiπ = cos(π) + i·sin(π)
Apply Euler's formula with θ = π
2
cos(π) = -1, sin(π) = 0
At 180°, we're on the left side of the circle
3
eiπ = -1 + 0i = -1
The point is at -1 on the real axis
4
eiπ + 1 = 0
Add 1 to both sides: -1 + 1 = 0 ✓

eiπ = -1 because walking π radians (180°) around the unit circle takes you to the point -1.

The Deeper Meaning

Think about what this really says:

  • eiθ means "walk θ radians around the circle"
  • eiπ means "walk halfway around" = end up at -1
  • ei2π means "walk all the way around" = end up at 1

The exponential function ex naturally creates rotation when the exponent is imaginary. This isn't a coincidence — it emerges from the mathematics of growth and change.

Why Does e Do This?

The number e ≈ 2.718... is defined by continuous compound growth. When you grow at an imaginary rate (a rate that involves i), instead of growing outward, you rotate.

eiθ = limn→∞ (1 + iθ/n)n
Growth in the imaginary direction = rotation

Each tiny step of "growing" by iθ/n adds a tiny rotation. As n approaches infinity, these tiny rotations smooth out into walking along the circle.

The Unity of Mathematics

Euler's identity shows that:

  • Exponentials (e) and trigonometry (cos, sin) are the same thing
  • Imaginary numbers (i) are just rotation by 90°
  • Pi (π) measures how far around the circle — halfway = opposite
  • 1 and -1 are the same point, separated by half a rotation

Euler's identity isn't a strange coincidence.
It's the inevitable consequence of rotation being the foundation of mathematics.
