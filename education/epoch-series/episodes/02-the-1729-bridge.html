I. THE FAMOUS STORY

In 1919, the mathematician G.H. Hardy visited his colleague Srinivasa Ramanujan in a London hospital. Ramanujan was dying of tuberculosis, far from his home in Tamil Nadu, India. He had only a year left to live.

Hardy, making small talk, mentioned that his taxi had a rather dull number: 1729.

Ramanujan's response became one of the most famous anecdotes in mathematical history:

"No, Hardy, it is a very interesting number. It is the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two different ways." — Srinivasa Ramanujan, 1919
1729 = 1³ + 12³ = 9³ + 10³
1729 = 1 + 1728 = 729 + 1000

Hardy had said "1729." Ramanujan, lying ill in a hospital bed, instantly recognized this unique property. He didn't calculate it — he SAW it. The number was already in his mind, filed under its properties, waiting to be recognized.

For a century, mathematicians have treated this as a charming story about Ramanujan's genius and his unusual relationship with numbers. They study 1729 as a "taxicab number" — the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two ways.

They missed the point entirely.
