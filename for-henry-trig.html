Unit 1: The Unit Circle

THE SECRET

Trig is not about triangles. It's about rotation.
sin = how HIGH the point is. cos = how far RIGHT the point is. That's it. Coordinates of a spinning point.

90° (π/2) | (0,1) | 180° (π) -----(−1,0)-----(1,0)---- 0° (0) | (0,−1) | 270° (3π/2) At any angle θ: cos(θ) = x-coordinate sin(θ) = y-coordinate
SPECIAL ANGLES
θ = 0°: (1, 0) cos=1, sin=0 θ = 30°: (√3/2, 1/2) cos=√3/2, sin=1/2 θ = 45°: (√2/2, √2/2) cos=√2/2, sin=√2/2 θ = 60°: (1/2, √3/2) cos=1/2, sin=√3/2 θ = 90°: (0, 1) cos=0, sin=1 Pattern: 30° and 60° SWAP their sin/cos values!
RADIANS ↔ DEGREES
180° = π radians To convert: degrees → radians: multiply by π/180 radians → degrees: multiply by 180/π Common values: 30° = π/6 45° = π/4 60° = π/3 90° = π/2 120° = 2π/3 180° = π 270° = 3π/2 360° = 2π
THE UNIT CIRCLE — FULL MAP π/2 (90°) (0, 1) | 2π/3 | π/3 (−½,√3/2) | (½,√3/2) \ | / 3π/4 \ | / π/4 (−√2/2, \ | / (√2/2, √2/2) \ | / √2/2) \ | / 5π/6 \|/ π/6 (−√3/2,½)----.----(√3/2,½) /|\ π -------/-|-\------- 0 (−1,0) / | \ (1,0) / | \ 7π/6 / | \ 11π/6 (−√3/2, / | \ (√3/2, −½) / | \ −½) / | \ 5π/4 | 7π/4 (−√2/2, | (√2/2, −√2/2) | −√2/2) \ | / 4π/3 \ | / 5π/3 (−½, \ | / (½, −√3/2) \ | / −√3/2) \|/ 3π/2 (270°) (0, −1)
THE PATTERN

Only THREE numbers matter: 0, 1/2, √2/2, √3/2, 1
The signs (+ or −) depend on quadrant. That's all.

Unit 2: The Six Trig Functions

THE SIX FUNCTIONS
PRIMARY (from the circle): sin θ = y = opposite/hypotenuse cos θ = x = adjacent/hypotenuse RATIOS: tan θ = sin/cos = opposite/adjacent = SLOPE cot θ = cos/sin = adjacent/opposite = 1/tan RECIPROCALS: sec θ = 1/cos = hypotenuse/adjacent csc θ = 1/sin = hypotenuse/opposite

Graphs of Trig Functions

SINUSOIDAL FORM
y = A sin(B(x - C)) + D y = A cos(B(x - C)) + D A = amplitude (vertical stretch) B = frequency (horizontal compress) C = phase shift (horizontal slide) D = vertical shift (midline) Period = 2π/|B|
GRAPHING STRATEGY

1. Find the period: 2π/B
2. Find the midline: y = D
3. Find amplitude: |A|
4. Mark the phase shift: start at x = C
5. Plot key points: start, max, middle, min, end

TANGENT & COTANGENT
tan θ: Period = π, asymptotes where cos=0 Asymptotes at π/2, 3π/2, ... Passes through origin cot θ: Period = π, asymptotes where sin=0 Asymptotes at 0, π, 2π, ...
SECANT & COSECANT
sec θ = 1/cos θ Asymptotes where cos=0 (at π/2, 3π/2, ...) U-shapes between asymptotes csc θ = 1/sin θ Asymptotes where sin=0 (at 0, π, 2π, ...) U-shapes between asymptotes

Unit 3: Trig Identities

MULTIPLICATIVE IDENTITY IS YOUR FRIEND

You can always multiply by 1 without changing anything.
1 comes in many disguises:
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1  |  sec²θ − tan²θ = 1  |  a/a = 1

PYTHAGOREAN IDENTITIES
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 ← THE fundamental identity Divide by cos²θ: tan²θ + 1 = sec²θ Divide by sin²θ: 1 + cot²θ = csc²θ
RECIPROCAL IDENTITIES
csc θ = 1/sin θ sec θ = 1/cos θ cot θ = 1/tan θ sin θ · csc θ = 1 cos θ · sec θ = 1 tan θ · cot θ = 1
QUOTIENT IDENTITIES
tan θ = sin θ / cos θ cot θ = cos θ / sin θ
EVEN/ODD IDENTITIES
sin(−θ) = −sin θ (odd function) cos(−θ) = cos θ (even function) tan(−θ) = −tan θ (odd function)
COFUNCTION IDENTITIES
sin(π/2 − θ) = cos θ cos(π/2 − θ) = sin θ tan(π/2 − θ) = cot θ Complementary angles have swapped functions
SUM & DIFFERENCE FORMULAS
sin(A + B) = sin A cos B + cos A sin B sin(A − B) = sin A cos B − cos A sin B cos(A + B) = cos A cos B − sin A sin B cos(A − B) = cos A cos B + sin A sin B tan(A + B) = (tan A + tan B)/(1 − tan A tan B) tan(A − B) = (tan A − tan B)/(1 + tan A tan B) sin = MIXED terms (sin×cos), cos = MATCHED terms
DOUBLE ANGLE FORMULAS
sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ cos(2θ) = cos²θ − sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 = 1 − 2sin²θ tan(2θ) = 2tan θ / (1 − tan²θ)
HALF ANGLE FORMULAS
sin(θ/2) = ±√[(1 − cos θ)/2] cos(θ/2) = ±√[(1 + cos θ)/2] tan(θ/2) = (1 − cos θ)/sin θ = sin θ/(1 + cos θ) ± depends on which quadrant θ/2 is in
POWER REDUCING FORMULAS
sin²θ = (1 − cos 2θ)/2 cos²θ = (1 + cos 2θ)/2 tan²θ = (1 − cos 2θ)/(1 + cos 2θ)
PRODUCT TO SUM
sin A cos B = ½[sin(A+B) + sin(A−B)] cos A sin B = ½[sin(A+B) − sin(A−B)] cos A cos B = ½[cos(A+B) + cos(A−B)] sin A sin B = ½[cos(A−B) − cos(A+B)]
SUM TO PRODUCT
sin A + sin B = 2 sin((A+B)/2) cos((A−B)/2) sin A − sin B = 2 cos((A+B)/2) sin((A−B)/2) cos A + cos B = 2 cos((A+B)/2) cos((A−B)/2) cos A − cos B = −2 sin((A+B)/2) sin((A−B)/2)

Unit 4: Solving Trig Equations

1 ISOLATE — Get trig function alone on one side
2 SIMPLIFY — Use identities to reduce to ONE function
3 SOLVE — Find reference angle
4 EXPAND — Find ALL solutions in [0, 2π)
5 GENERALIZE — Add + 2πn for general solution
EXAMPLE

Solve: 2sin²θ − 1 = 0 for θ in [0, 2π)

SOLUTION

2sin²θ = 1 → sin²θ = 1/2 → sin θ = ±√2/2

Reference angle: π/4 (where sin = √2/2)

All quadrants: sin = +√2/2 in Q1, Q2 → π/4, 3π/4

sin = −√2/2 in Q3, Q4 → 5π/4, 7π/4

θ = π/4, 3π/4, 5π/4, 7π/4

FACTORING PATTERNS
Look for these: sin²θ − 1 = (sin θ + 1)(sin θ − 1) 2cos²θ + cos θ − 1 = (2cos θ − 1)(cos θ + 1) u-substitution: Let u = sin θ, solve quadratic, then solve for θ

Unit 5: Triangle Trigonometry

LAW OF SINES
a b c ───── = ───── = ───── sin A sin B sin C Use when you know: • AAS (angle-angle-side) • ASA (angle-side-angle) • SSA (side-side-angle) — watch for ambiguous case!
LAW OF COSINES
c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C b² = a² + c² − 2ac cos B a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A Use when you know: • SAS (side-angle-side) • SSS (all three sides) This is the generalized Pythagorean theorem!
AREA OF A TRIANGLE
Area = ½ · a · b · sin C (Half the product of two sides times sine of included angle) Also: Area = ½ · base · height Heron's formula (SSS): s = (a+b+c)/2 (semi-perimeter) Area = √[s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)]
AMBIGUOUS CASE (SSA)

When given SSA, there may be 0, 1, or 2 triangles!
Use Law of Sines to find the angle. If sin > 1: no triangle.
If angle could be acute OR obtuse: two triangles possible.

Unit 6: Inverse Trig Functions

INVERSE FUNCTIONS
arcsin (sin⁻¹): "What angle has this sine?" Domain: [−1, 1] Range: [−π/2, π/2] arccos (cos⁻¹): "What angle has this cosine?" Domain: [−1, 1] Range: [0, π] arctan (tan⁻¹): "What angle has this tangent?" Domain: (−∞, ∞) Range: (−π/2, π/2)
CRITICAL CONCEPT

Inverse trig gives you the principal value — ONE answer in the restricted range.
For all solutions, you must consider other quadrants!

COMPOSITIONS
sin(arcsin x) = x for x ∈ [−1, 1] cos(arccos x) = x for x ∈ [−1, 1] tan(arctan x) = x for all x arcsin(sin x) = x only if x ∈ [−π/2, π/2] arccos(cos x) = x only if x ∈ [0, π] arctan(tan x) = x only if x ∈ (−π/2, π/2)
EXAMPLE

Find cos(arcsin(3/5))

SOLUTION

Let θ = arcsin(3/5), so sin θ = 3/5

Since θ is in [−π/2, π/2], cos θ ≥ 0

Use identity: cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ = 1 − 9/25 = 16/25

cos θ = 4/5

Unit 7: Polar Coordinates

POLAR vs RECTANGULAR y ↑ | • (x, y) = (r, θ) | / | / r | / | / θ ─────────+─────────→ x | x = r cos θ y = r sin θ
CONVERSIONS
Polar → Rectangular: x = r cos θ y = r sin θ Rectangular → Polar: r = √(x² + y²) θ = arctan(y/x) (adjust quadrant!)
COMMON POLAR CURVES
r = a Circle (radius a) r = a cos θ Circle through origin r = a sin θ Circle through origin r = a + b cos θ Limaçon (cardioid if a=b) r = a cos(nθ) Rose curve (n petals if odd, 2n if even) r² = a² cos(2θ) Lemniscate (figure 8) r = aθ Spiral of Archimedes
RAMANUJAN CONNECTION

The polar form is where circles become algebra.
r = 1 IS the unit circle. θ is the angle.
This connects directly to e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ

Unit 8: Complex Numbers

FORMS OF COMPLEX NUMBERS
Rectangular: z = a + bi Polar/Trig: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) Exponential: z = re^(iθ) where: r = |z| = √(a² + b²) (modulus) θ = arg(z) = arctan(b/a) (argument)
EULER'S FORMULA — THE MOST BEAUTIFUL EQUATION

e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ

At θ = π: e^(iπ) + 1 = 0
Five fundamental constants in one equation.

OPERATIONS IN POLAR FORM
Multiplication: z₁ · z₂ = r₁r₂ · e^(i(θ₁+θ₂)) = r₁r₂[cos(θ₁+θ₂) + i sin(θ₁+θ₂)] Division: z₁/z₂ = (r₁/r₂) · e^(i(θ₁−θ₂)) Power (De Moivre's Theorem): zⁿ = rⁿ · e^(inθ) = rⁿ[cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)]
ROOTS OF COMPLEX NUMBERS
The n-th roots of z = re^(iθ): w_k = r^(1/n) · e^(i(θ + 2πk)/n) for k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n−1 The roots are evenly spaced around a circle!

Unit 9: Vectors

VECTOR BASICS
Component form: v = ⟨a, b⟩ or v = ai + bj Magnitude: |v| = √(a² + b²) Direction angle: θ = arctan(b/a) Unit vector: v̂ = v/|v| = ⟨a/|v|, b/|v|⟩
VECTOR OPERATIONS
Addition: ⟨a₁, b₁⟩ + ⟨a₂, b₂⟩ = ⟨a₁+a₂, b₁+b₂⟩ Scalar multiplication: k⟨a, b⟩ = ⟨ka, kb⟩ Dot product: v · w = a₁a₂ + b₁b₂ = |v||w|cos θ Cross product (3D): v × w = |v||w|sin θ · n̂
ANGLE BETWEEN VECTORS
cos θ = (v · w)/(|v| |w|) θ = arccos[(v · w)/(|v| |w|)] Perpendicular vectors: v · w = 0 Parallel vectors: v = kw for some scalar k
PROJECTIONS
Scalar projection of v onto w: comp_w v = (v · w)/|w| Vector projection of v onto w: proj_w v = [(v · w)/|w|²] · w
APPLICATIONS

Work: W = F · d = |F||d|cos θ
Torque: τ = r × F = |r||F|sin θ

Unit 10: Parametric Equations

PARAMETRIC FORM
x = f(t) y = g(t) t = parameter (often time) To eliminate parameter: Solve one equation for t Substitute into the other
COMMON PARAMETRIC CURVES
Circle: x = r cos t, y = r sin t, t ∈ [0, 2π] Ellipse: x = a cos t, y = b sin t, t ∈ [0, 2π] Line: x = x₀ + at, y = y₀ + bt Projectile motion: x = v₀ cos(θ) · t y = v₀ sin(θ) · t − ½gt²
CALCULUS WITH PARAMETRICS
dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) Arc length: s = ∫√[(dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²] dt
WHY PARAMETRIC?

Some curves can't be written as y = f(x).
Parametric lets you trace curves with direction and time.
Physics uses parametric equations everywhere.
