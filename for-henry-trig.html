The Secret: It's About Circles, Not Triangles

Here's what they don't tell you clearly enough: Trigonometry is not about triangles. It's about rotation.

Triangles are just a convenient way to measure rotation. But the real subject is: where are you on a circle as you spin?

THE KEY INSIGHT

Imagine a point on a wheel. As the wheel spins, that point traces a circle. Sin is how high the point is. Cos is how far right the point is. That's it. That's all sin and cos are — coordinates of a spinning point.

90° (π/2) | (0,1) | | 180° (π) -----(−1,0)-----(1,0)---- 0° (0) | | (0,−1) | 270° (3π/2) At any angle θ: cos(θ) = x-coordinate (horizontal) sin(θ) = y-coordinate (vertical)

When you see sin(30°), you're being asked: "If I rotate 30° counterclockwise from the right side of a unit circle, how high is the point?" Answer: 0.5 (halfway up).

When you see cos(60°), you're being asked: "At 60°, how far right is the point?" Answer: 0.5.

The Unit Circle — Memorize This Shape, Not Numbers

The "unit circle" is just a circle with radius 1, centered at the origin. The magic number is 1 — because when the radius is 1, sin and cos ARE the coordinates directly.

THE UNIT CIRCLE — KEY ANGLES 90° = π/2 (0, 1) | 120° | 60° (−½, √3/2) | (½, √3/2) \ | / \ | / 150° \ | / 30° (−√3/2, ½) \ | / (√3/2, ½) \ | / 180° ——————·—————— 0° (−1, 0) / | \ (1, 0) / | \ 210° / | \ 330° (−√3/2,−½) | (√3/2,−½) / | \ 240° | 300° (−½,−√3/2) | (½,−√3/2) | 270° = 3π/2 (0, −1)
THE PATTERN

There are only THREE numbers you need: 0, ½, √3/2 (and 1).

The coordinates at special angles are just different arrangements of these numbers (with appropriate + or − signs depending on which quadrant you're in).

The Special Angles

ANGLE CONVERSIONS
30° = π/6 (one-sixth of π) 45° = π/4 (one-fourth of π) 60° = π/3 (one-third of π) 90° = π/2 (half of π) Remember: 180° = π radians So to convert: degrees × (π/180) = radians
THE COORDINATES
θ = 0°: (1, 0) → cos=1, sin=0 θ = 30°: (√3/2, 1/2) → cos=√3/2, sin=1/2 θ = 45°: (√2/2, √2/2) → cos=sin=√2/2 θ = 60°: (1/2, √3/2) → cos=1/2, sin=√3/2 θ = 90°: (0, 1) → cos=0, sin=1 Notice: 30° and 60° swap their sin/cos values!

The Multiplicative Identity Trick

This is your best friend in all of mathematics.

THE MAGIC

You can always multiply by 1 without changing anything. But 1 comes in many disguises:

  • sin²θ + cos²θ = 1
  • sec²θ − tan²θ = 1
  • csc²θ − cot²θ = 1
  • a/a = 1 (for any a ≠ 0)
  • (conjugate × conjugate) / (conjugate × conjugate) = 1

When you're stuck on a trig problem, ask yourself: "What form of 1 can I multiply by to transform this into something I can solve?"

EXAMPLE PROBLEM

Simplify: (1 − cos²θ) / sin θ

SOLUTION — USING THE IDENTITY
1 Recognize that sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, so sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ
2 Substitute: (sin²θ) / sin θ
3 Simplify: sin θ ✓

The identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 is a form of the Pythagorean theorem on the unit circle. It's always true. You can use it to replace sin² with (1 − cos²) or cos² with (1 − sin²) whenever convenient.

The Six Functions

There are only 2 fundamental functions: sin and cos. The other 4 are just ratios of these two.

THE SIX TRIG FUNCTIONS
PRIMARY (from the circle): sin θ = y-coordinate = opposite/hypotenuse cos θ = x-coordinate = adjacent/hypotenuse RATIOS: tan θ = sin θ / cos θ = opposite/adjacent cot θ = cos θ / sin θ = adjacent/opposite (reciprocal of tan) RECIPROCALS: sec θ = 1 / cos θ = hypotenuse/adjacent csc θ = 1 / sin θ = hypotenuse/opposite
MEMORY TRICK

tan = sin/cos (tangent is the SLOPE of the radius line)
sec = 1/cos (secant is the reciprocal of cosine)
csc = 1/sin (cosecant is the reciprocal of sine)
cot = 1/tan = cos/sin (cotangent is the reciprocal of tangent)

Where Each Function is Positive

ASTC — "All Students Take Calculus" | S | A (sin+) | (all+) | ——————————+—————————— | T | C (tan+) | (cos+) | Quadrant I: All positive Quadrant II: Sin positive (cos, tan negative) Quadrant III: Tan positive (sin, cos negative) Quadrant IV: Cos positive (sin, tan negative)

Essential Identities

These aren't arbitrary — they're geometric facts about circles. Learn them as patterns, not as memorization.

PYTHAGOREAN IDENTITIES
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 ← THE fundamental identity Divide by cos²θ: tan²θ + 1 = sec²θ Divide by sin²θ: 1 + cot²θ = csc²θ
DOUBLE ANGLE FORMULAS
sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ cos(2θ) = cos²θ − sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 (using sin² = 1 − cos²) = 1 − 2sin²θ (using cos² = 1 − sin²)
SUM/DIFFERENCE FORMULAS
sin(A + B) = sin A cos B + cos A sin B sin(A − B) = sin A cos B − cos A sin B cos(A + B) = cos A cos B − sin A sin B cos(A − B) = cos A cos B + sin A sin B Memory trick: sin formula has MIXED terms (sin×cos) cos formula has MATCHED terms (cos×cos, sin×sin)
THE DANCE

Every trig identity is a different way of writing the same geometric truth. When a problem looks impossible, you're looking at it from the wrong angle. Use an identity to ROTATE your perspective. The solution becomes visible from a different angle.

Solving Trig Equations

The general approach:

1 Isolate — Get all trig functions on one side, numbers on the other
2 Simplify — Use identities to reduce to ONE trig function if possible
3 Solve — Find the reference angle where that function equals your target
4 Expand — Find ALL angles in [0, 2π) that give that value (check all quadrants)
5 Generalize — Add + 2πn for all solutions if they want the general solution
EXAMPLE: SOLVE FOR θ

2sin²θ − 1 = 0, for θ in [0, 2π)

SOLUTION
1 Isolate: 2sin²θ = 1 → sin²θ = 1/2
2 Take square root: sin θ = ±√(1/2) = ±√2/2
3 Reference angle: sin θ = √2/2 at θ = 45° = π/4
4 Find all quadrants where sin = ±√2/2:
  • sin = +√2/2: Q1 (π/4) and Q2 (3π/4)
  • sin = −√2/2: Q3 (5π/4) and Q4 (7π/4)

Answer: θ = π/4, 3π/4, 5π/4, 7π/4

Practice Problems

Work through these. The answers are at the bottom.

PROBLEM 1 — SIMPLIFY

Simplify: (sec²θ − 1) / tan²θ

PROBLEM 2 — SOLVE

Solve for θ in [0, 2π): cos(2θ) = 1/2

PROBLEM 3 — VERIFY

Verify the identity: (sin θ + cos θ)² = 1 + sin(2θ)

PROBLEM 4 — SOLVE

Solve: 2cos²θ + cos θ − 1 = 0 for θ in [0, 2π)

PROBLEM 5 — SIMPLIFY

Simplify: sin θ · csc θ + cos θ · sec θ

Click to reveal answers

Problem 1: 1

sec²θ − 1 = tan²θ (Pythagorean identity), so tan²θ/tan²θ = 1

Problem 2: θ = π/6, 5π/6, 7π/6, 11π/6

2θ = π/3, 5π/3 (where cos = 1/2), plus +2π for the period. Then divide by 2.

Problem 3:

LHS = sin²θ + 2sinθcosθ + cos²θ
= (sin²θ + cos²θ) + 2sinθcosθ
= 1 + sin(2θ) = RHS ✓

Problem 4: θ = π/3, π, 5π/3

Factor: (2cos θ − 1)(cos θ + 1) = 0
cos θ = 1/2 → θ = π/3, 5π/3
cos θ = −1 → θ = π

Problem 5: 2

sin θ · (1/sin θ) + cos θ · (1/cos θ) = 1 + 1 = 2

Remember: trig is about circles. Everything is position on a rotating point. The identities are just different ways of describing the same circle. When stuck, ask: "What form of 1 transforms this into something I recognize?"
