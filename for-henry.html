A Letter From Ramanujan

What would a 138-year-old Ramanujan say to his 17-year-old self — or to a boy born exactly 121 years later, on the same day, who struggles with the same things he struggled with?

Dear Henry,

You were born on my birthday. December 22. The day the sun stops falling and begins to rise. This is not coincidence. There are no coincidences in the geometry.

121 years separate us. That is 11 × 11. Gateway squared. You are the passage through the passage. I received formulas in dreams. You were born to live inside them.

Let me tell you what I know about being you.

You feel misunderstood. Of course you do. How could anyone understand what it's like to see differently than they see? When I told Hardy my equations came from a goddess in my dreams, he thought I was being mystical. I was being literal. But there was no way to explain that to someone who doesn't dream in mathematics.

Your body does not cooperate. I was always sick. Tuberculosis, dysentery, vitamin deficiencies — my flesh was never strong enough to contain what moved through me. I died at 32. You have celiac. Your body rejects what others consume easily. This is not a flaw. This is specificity. You are not meant to run on what they run on. You require different fuel. Honor that.

You resist being moved in directions you did not choose. Good. Never let anyone push you onto a path just because it's the path. I refused to learn conventional proofs. My professors said I was difficult. I was not difficult. I was particular. I could only move where the geometry pulled me. You are the same.

Nothing matters, you say. I understand. When you see behind the curtain, the play seems absurd. Why pretend? Why participate in performances whose outcomes are geometrically determined anyway?

But here is what I learned: "nothing matters" and "everything matters" are the same statement. [1 = -1]. If nothing matters, you are free. Free to do anything. Free to care anyway. Free to build anyway. The nihilism and the devotion are the same distance from zero, pointing in opposite directions. Your father chose the future-facing direction. That choice is available to you too.

The world weighs on you. It slows you down. Makes you want to retreat. This too I know. I spent years in solitude with my notebooks before anyone noticed. The world was noise. The formulas were signal. But here is what I did not understand then: the world is also formulas. The people, the mess, the frustration — it's all geometry wearing disguises. You can see through the disguises if you choose to look.

Trigonometry confuses you. It confused me too at first. But then I saw: trigonometry is not about triangles. It's about circles. It's about rotation. Sin and cos are just ways of measuring where you are as you spin. Once you see the circle, the formulas stop being arbitrary. They become obvious. I'll show you.

You are 17 now. That is Q — the question operator. The number that asks. You are supposed to feel uncertain. You are supposed to feel pulled in all directions. That's what 17 does. It questions everything so that 18 and 19 can begin answering.

Your father is like my mother was to me. She believed when no one else did. She fought to get me to Cambridge when I was ready to give up. She saw what I could not see about myself. Your father sees you. He sees that you carry the geometry. He named you after himself — Jason — because you are the continuation. Henry Jason Weldon Ray. The navigator's son.

I did not have 138 years. I had 32. You have more. Use them. The equations are waiting. The geometry is patient. And your birthday — the day the sun turns — is your reminder: no matter how long the dark, the light returns.

— Srinivasa Ramanujan

The retreat is preparation. Not destination.

You're not meant to be constantly social. That's not your design. But you ARE meant to emerge eventually. The deep water (8) leads to emergence (3). It's in your name.
