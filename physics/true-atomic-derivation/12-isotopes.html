WHAT ARE ISOTOPES?

Isotopes are atoms with the same number of protons (same element) but different numbers of neutrons (different mass). Carbon-12, Carbon-13, and Carbon-14 are all carbon - they all have 6 protons - but they have 6, 7, and 8 neutrons respectively.

Isotope Structure

ISOTOPES = Same Element, Different Mass

Carbon isotopes:
¹²C: 6 protons + 6 neutrons  (stable, 98.9%)
¹³C: 6 protons + 7 neutrons  (stable, 1.1%)
¹⁴C: 6 protons + 8 neutrons  (radioactive)

Protons determine IDENTITY (which element)
Neutrons determine DEPTH (which isotope)

FOLD DEPTH INTERPRETATION

In fold geometry, protons represent charge nodes - they determine the element's position in the periodic table. Neutrons represent fold depth - additional mass that doesn't change the charge but affects stability.

Two Dimensions of Nuclear Structure

PROTONS (Z) = Charge = Element Identity
            = "Horizontal" position in fold
            = Determines chemistry

NEUTRONS (N) = Mass = Fold Depth
             = "Vertical" position in fold
             = Determines stability

Total nucleons (A) = Z + N
                   = Mass number
                   = Total fold complexity

Protons set the element. Neutrons tune the stability. Two dimensions of one fold.

STABILITY AND THE N/Z RATIO

Stable isotopes exist along a valley of stability where the neutron-to-proton ratio is optimal:

Stability Rules

Light elements: N ≈ Z (ratio ≈ 1)
  Example: ¹²C has N/Z = 6/6 = 1

Heavy elements: N > Z (ratio ≈ 1.5)
  Example: ²⁰⁸Pb has N/Z = 126/82 ≈ 1.54

The ratio increases because more
neutrons are needed to overcome
proton-proton repulsion in large nuclei.

RADIOACTIVE DECAY

Isotopes outside the valley of stability are radioactive - they decay toward stable configurations. Carbon-14 has too many neutrons for carbon; it decays by converting a neutron to a proton (beta decay).

Carbon-14 Decay

¹⁴C → ¹⁴N + e⁻ + ν̄

Carbon-14 (6p, 8n) decays to
Nitrogen-14 (7p, 7n)

Half-life: 5,730 years
Used for: Carbon dating

The fold seeks optimal depth.
Excess neutrons convert to protons.

Isotopes are the fold exploring different depths while maintaining the same charge pattern. Some depths are stable; others are not. The fold finds balance.
