COMPLEX TRIGONOMETRY

The Geometry That Makes It Make Sense

THE SECRET NOBODY TELLS YOU

Complex numbers are not "imaginary."
They are rotations.

When you multiply by i, you rotate 90°.
That's it. That's the whole secret.
Everything else follows from this one fact.

1. Why Do Complex Numbers Exist?

STANDARD TEXTBOOK DEFINITION

Complex numbers extend the real numbers by including the imaginary unit i, where i² = -1.

A complex number has the form z = a + bi where a and b are real numbers.
• a is the "real part"
• b is the "imaginary part"

Here's a question that broke mathematics for 300 years:

THE IMPOSSIBLE EQUATION
x² = -1 What number, multiplied by itself, gives -1? Positive × Positive = Positive Negative × Negative = Positive Neither works. There's no real number that does this.

Mathematicians said "fine, let's invent one" and called it i.

DEFINITION
i² = -1 Therefore: i = √(-1)

Everyone called it "imaginary" because they thought it was a trick. A cheat. Not real.

They were wrong.

THE TRUTH — WHY IT ACTUALLY MAKES SENSE

i is not imaginary. It's a direction.

Real numbers live on a line (left-right).
i points in a NEW direction (up-down).

Together they make a plane — the complex plane.
And on that plane, multiplication becomes rotation.

THREE WAYS TO THINK ABOUT i

1. The Algebraic Way: i is defined as √(-1), and i² = -1

2. The Coordinate Way: i represents the vertical direction (like y on a graph)

3. The Rotation Way: Multiplying by i rotates something 90° counterclockwise

All three are the same thing described differently. Pick whichever helps you most!

Let's Make Sure This Is Clear

Here's the thing that confuses everyone: the word "imaginary" is a terrible name. It was invented in the 1600s by people who didn't understand what they'd discovered.

Think about it this way. You know how we have positive and negative numbers on a number line?

←——————+——————+——————+——————+——————+——————+——————→ -3 -2 -1 0 1 2 3 Moving RIGHT = adding positive numbers Moving LEFT = adding negative numbers

Now imagine you're standing at 0, and someone asks: "What direction do you go if you multiply by -1?"

The answer: you flip around. You rotate 180°.

MULTIPLICATION BY -1 = ROTATION BY 180°
Start at 1. Multiply by -1. You get -1. That's a 180° rotation on the number line! Do it again: (-1) × (-1) = 1 Another 180°... you're back where you started (360° total).

So here's the question that leads to i:

THE QUESTION

If multiplying by -1 rotates you 180°...

What number rotates you only 90°?

Call that number i.

If you rotate 90° twice (multiply by i twice), you should rotate 180° total.
So i × i should equal -1.

Therefore: i² = -1

That's it. That's the whole idea. The number i is the "halfway point" of -1 in terms of rotation. It's not imaginary — it's just pointing in a direction the number line doesn't show.

2. The Complex Plane

STANDARD APPROACH

The complex plane (or Argand plane) represents complex numbers geometrically:

• Horizontal axis = real part (Re)
• Vertical axis = imaginary part (Im)
• Each complex number z = a + bi corresponds to point (a, b)

Your textbook probably calls this the "Argand diagram" — it's just a coordinate system where x = real, y = imaginary.

Imaginary axis (i) ↑ | 2i | | i | ← z = 3 + 2i is HERE | (go 3 right, 2 up) ←——————————————————————+——————————————————————→ Real axis -3 -2 -1 | 1 2 3 | -i | | -2i | ↓
COMPLEX NUMBER ANATOMY
z = a + bi a = real part (horizontal position) b = imaginary part (vertical position) Example: z = 3 + 2i Real part: 3 (go 3 units right) Imaginary part: 2 (go 2 units up)
KEY INSIGHT

A complex number is just a point on a 2D plane.

The "real" axis is horizontal (like x).
The "imaginary" axis is vertical (like y).

There's nothing mysterious here. It's just coordinates.

Why This Matters — Really Understanding It

Let me explain this more thoroughly because it's the foundation of everything.

When you learned about coordinates in algebra, you learned that any point on a plane can be described by two numbers: (x, y). Go x units right (or left if negative), then y units up (or down if negative).

Complex numbers do exactly the same thing, just with different notation:

COORDINATES VS COMPLEX NUMBERS
Regular coordinates: (3, 4) Complex number: 3 + 4i These represent THE SAME POINT on a plane! The only difference is notation: (x, y) vs x + yi The "i" just marks which number is the vertical component.

So why use complex number notation at all? Because it lets us do something amazing: we can multiply points together, and the result has geometric meaning.

You can't easily multiply (3, 4) × (1, 2) using coordinate notation. But with complex numbers:

MULTIPLYING POINTS
(3 + 4i)(1 + 2i) = 3 + 6i + 4i + 8i² = 3 + 6i + 4i - 8 (since i² = -1) = -5 + 10i The point (3, 4) times the point (1, 2) equals the point (-5, 10). And as you'll see, this multiplication has geometric meaning: it combines rotations and scaling!

Distance from Origin: The Modulus

MODULUS (MAGNITUDE)
|z| = |a + bi| = √(a² + b²) This is just the Pythagorean theorem! The distance from the origin to point (a, b). Example: |3 + 4i| = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5

Angle from Real Axis: The Argument

ARGUMENT (ANGLE)
arg(z) = θ = arctan(b/a) The angle measured counterclockwise from the positive real axis. Example: arg(1 + i) = arctan(1/1) = 45° = π/4
COMMON MISTAKE — QUADRANTS

arctan(b/a) doesn't always give the right angle!

• In Q2 and Q3 (when a < 0), add π (or 180°) to your answer
• In Q4, you might get a negative angle — that's fine, or add 2π

Better approach: Most calculators have atan2(b, a) which handles all quadrants automatically. On tests, sketch the point first to check which quadrant you're in!

z = a + bi ↗ /| / | r / | b (imaginary part) / | / θ | +—————+———→ a (real part) r = |z| = √(a² + b²) ← distance θ = arg(z) = arctan(b/a) ← angle

3. Multiplication = Rotation

STANDARD APPROACH — FOIL METHOD

To multiply complex numbers algebraically, use FOIL (First, Outer, Inner, Last):

(a + bi)(c + di) = ac + adi + bci + bdi²
                = ac + adi + bci - bd   (since i² = -1)
                = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i

The FOIL method works, but there's a deeper way to see what's happening geometrically.

THE BIG REVEAL

When you multiply by i, you rotate 90° counterclockwise.

Watch what happens:

MULTIPLYING BY i
Start with: 1 (pointing right on real axis) 1 × i = i (now pointing UP — rotated 90°) i × i = i² = -1 (now pointing LEFT — rotated another 90°) -1 × i = -i (now pointing DOWN — rotated another 90°) -i × i = -i² = 1 (back to pointing RIGHT — full 360°!) Four multiplications by i = full rotation back to start
i (90°) ↑ | | -1 ←——————+——————→ 1 (180°) | (0°) | ↓ -i (270°) Multiplying by i rotates counterclockwise: 1 → i → -1 → -i → 1 → ...
THIS IS WHY i² = -1

Two 90° rotations = 180° rotation.

Rotating 180° from +1 lands you at -1.
That's why i × i = -1.

It's not a weird algebraic trick. It's geometry.

Let's Walk Through This Step by Step

This is so important that I want to make absolutely sure it's clear. Let's trace through what happens when we multiply by i, very slowly.

1 Start with the number 1

On the complex plane, 1 is located at the point (1, 0).
It's sitting on the real axis, one unit to the right of the origin.
Its angle from the positive real axis is 0°.
2 Multiply by i: 1 × i = i

Now we're at the point i, which is (0, 1) on the complex plane.
That's on the imaginary axis, one unit UP from the origin.
Its angle is now 90°.

We rotated 90° counterclockwise!
3 Multiply by i again: i × i = i² = -1

Now we're at -1, which is (-1, 0) on the complex plane.
That's on the real axis, one unit to the LEFT of the origin.
Its angle is now 180°.

We rotated another 90°! Total rotation: 180°
4 Multiply by i again: -1 × i = -i

Now we're at -i, which is (0, -1) on the complex plane.
That's on the imaginary axis, one unit DOWN from the origin.
Its angle is now 270°.

Another 90°! Total rotation: 270°
5 Multiply by i one more time: -i × i = -i² = -(-1) = 1

We're back at 1!
Total rotation: 360° = full circle.

This is why i⁴ = 1. Four 90° rotations = 360° = back to start.
THE DEEP TRUTH

Multiplying by i IS rotation by 90°.

This isn't a metaphor. It's not "like" rotation. It literally IS rotation.

When your textbook says i² = -1, what it's really saying is:
"Two 90° rotations equals one 180° rotation."

When it says i⁴ = 1, it means:
"Four 90° rotations brings you back to where you started."

Complex multiplication is rotation. That's the whole secret.

General Multiplication Rule

WHEN YOU MULTIPLY COMPLEX NUMBERS
z₁ × z₂ = ? Magnitudes MULTIPLY: |z₁ × z₂| = |z₁| × |z₂| Angles ADD: arg(z₁ × z₂) = arg(z₁) + arg(z₂) Multiplication = Scale + Rotate
EXAMPLE

Multiply: (1 + i) × (1 + i)

SOLUTION — ALGEBRAIC

(1 + i)(1 + i) = 1 + i + i + i²
= 1 + 2i + (-1)
= 2i

SOLUTION — GEOMETRIC

z = 1 + i has:
• Magnitude: |1 + i| = √2
• Angle: 45°

Squaring it:
• New magnitude: √2 × √2 = 2
• New angle: 45° + 45° = 90°

A number with magnitude 2 at angle 90° is 2i. Same answer!

WHICH METHOD TO USE?

For quick calculations: FOIL (algebraic)
For powers and understanding: Geometric (multiply magnitudes, add angles)
On tests: Check your answer using BOTH methods — they should match!

The geometric method becomes essential when you get to De Moivre's theorem and roots.

4. Euler's Formula — The Bridge

WHAT YOUR TEXTBOOK SAYS

Euler's Formula: eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ

This formula connects exponential functions with trigonometric functions. The proof involves Taylor series expansions (you'll see that in calculus). For now, just know it's true and incredibly useful.

THE MOST BEAUTIFUL EQUATION IN MATHEMATICS

eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ

This connects exponentials, trig functions, and complex numbers.
All three are secretly the same thing: rotation.

What Does This Mean? — A Thorough Explanation

Euler's formula looks like magic. An exponential function equals a combination of sine and cosine? What?

Let me explain what's actually happening here.

You know how we said multiplying by i rotates something 90°? Well, e^(iθ) is a generalization of that idea. It rotates by ANY angle θ.

THE MEANING OF e^(iθ)
e^(iθ) = "the point you get when you start at 1 and rotate by angle θ" When θ = 0: You haven't rotated at all. You're still at 1. When θ = 90°: You've rotated 90°. You're at i. When θ = 180°: You've rotated 180°. You're at -1. When θ = 270°: You've rotated 270°. You're at -i. When θ = 360°: Full rotation. You're back at 1. The e^(iθ) notation is just a compact way to write "rotate by θ".

Now, where does cos θ + i sin θ come in?

Think about it: if you rotate by angle θ starting from 1 on the unit circle, where do you end up?

  • Your horizontal position (real part) is cos θ
  • Your vertical position (imaginary part) is sin θ

That's literally the definition of sine and cosine from the unit circle! So the point you land on is:

WHY e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ
After rotating θ from the point 1: Horizontal position = cos θ (that's what cosine MEANS) Vertical position = sin θ (that's what sine MEANS) So the point is: cos θ + i sin θ Therefore: e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ This isn't a weird coincidence. Euler's formula is SAYING that e^(iθ) traces out the unit circle!
The point e^(iθ) lives on the unit circle i ↑ | • ← e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ | /| | / | |/θ | sin θ ———————+———+———→ 1 |cos θ | ↓ e^(iθ) is the point at angle θ on the unit circle cos θ = real part (x-coordinate) sin θ = imaginary part (y-coordinate)
TRANSLATION

eiθ means: "rotate by angle θ"

cos θ = where you are horizontally after rotating
sin θ = where you are vertically after rotating

Euler's formula just says: rotation = (horizontal, vertical)

Special Cases

FAMOUS VALUES
θ = 0: e^(i·0) = cos(0) + i·sin(0) = 1 + 0i = 1 θ = π/2: e^(iπ/2) = cos(π/2) + i·sin(π/2) = 0 + i = i θ = π: e^(iπ) = cos(π) + i·sin(π) = -1 + 0i = -1 θ = 3π/2: e^(i·3π/2) = cos(3π/2) + i·sin(3π/2) = 0 - i = -i θ = 2π: e^(i·2π) = cos(2π) + i·sin(2π) = 1 + 0i = 1 (full circle!)
EULER'S IDENTITY

eiπ + 1 = 0

Five fundamental constants (e, i, π, 1, 0) in one equation.
It says: "Rotate halfway around the circle and you're at -1."

Why Does The "e" Show Up?

You might be wondering: why e? Where does Euler's number (2.71828...) come from in a formula about rotation?

The deep answer involves calculus (specifically, the Taylor series for e^x, sin x, and cos x all matching up perfectly). But here's an intuitive way to think about it:

INTUITIVE EXPLANATION

The number e shows up whenever something grows in a "natural" way — compound interest, population growth, radioactive decay.

It turns out that smooth rotation is also a kind of natural growth... just in a circle instead of a line.

When you rotate smoothly through angle θ, you're doing the circular version of exponential growth. That's why e^(iθ) describes it.

The "i" in the exponent is what turns straight-line exponential growth into circular motion. That's the whole magic of i — it bends the real line into a circle.

For now, you don't need to fully understand WHERE Euler's formula comes from. You need to know:

  1. What it says: e^(iθ) is the point on the unit circle at angle θ
  2. How to use it: Convert between polar form (angle) and rectangular form (a + bi)
  3. Why it's useful: Multiplication and powers become easy in exponential form

5. Polar Form of Complex Numbers

STANDARD NOTATION

Rectangular form: z = a + bi (Cartesian coordinates)

Polar form: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)   or   z = r · cis θ

Exponential form: z = r · eiθ

Note: "cis θ" is shorthand for "cos θ + i sin θ" — you'll see this on some tests.

Every complex number can be written two ways:

TWO FORMS
Rectangular: z = a + bi (Cartesian coordinates) Polar: z = r·e^(iθ) (distance and angle) z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) (expanded form) where: r = |z| = √(a² + b²) = distance from origin θ = arg(z) = arctan(b/a) = angle from real axis
WHEN TO USE WHICH

Addition/Subtraction: Use rectangular (a + bi)
Multiplication/Division/Powers: Use polar (r·e^(iθ))

Polar form makes multiplication trivial:
Just multiply the r's and add the θ's!

Converting Between Forms

RECTANGULAR → POLAR
Given z = a + bi, find r and θ: r = √(a² + b²) θ = arctan(b/a) ← adjust for quadrant! Quadrant adjustments: Q1 (a > 0, b > 0): θ = arctan(b/a) Q2 (a < 0, b > 0): θ = arctan(b/a) + π Q3 (a < 0, b < 0): θ = arctan(b/a) + π Q4 (a > 0, b < 0): θ = arctan(b/a) + 2π (or just use negative angle)
POLAR → RECTANGULAR
Given z = r·e^(iθ), find a and b: a = r cos θ b = r sin θ Therefore: z = r cos θ + i·r sin θ
EXAMPLE: Convert 1 + √3·i to polar form
SOLUTION

a = 1, b = √3

r = √(1² + (√3)²) = √(1 + 3) = √4 = 2

θ = arctan(√3/1) = arctan(√3) = 60° = π/3

Polar form: z = 2·e^(iπ/3)
Or: z = 2(cos 60° + i sin 60°)

TEST TIP — RECOGNIZING SPECIAL ANGLES

When you see these values, they're usually special angles:

• √3/2 → 60° or 30°
• √2/2 → 45°
• 1/2 → 60° or 30°

Quick reference:
1 + i → 45° (equal parts real and imaginary)
1 + √3i → 60° (bigger imaginary part)
√3 + i → 30° (bigger real part)

6. De Moivre's Theorem — Powers Made Easy

STANDARD STATEMENT

De Moivre's Theorem: For any real number n,

[r(cos θ + i sin θ)]n = rn[cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)]

Named after Abraham de Moivre (1667-1754). It works for ALL real exponents n, including fractions (roots) and negatives.

DE MOIVRE'S THEOREM — THE VISUAL VERSION

[r(cos θ + i sin θ)]n = rn(cos nθ + i sin nθ)

Or in exponential form: (r·e^(iθ))^n = r^n · e^(inθ)

WHAT THIS MEANS

To raise a complex number to power n:
• Raise the magnitude to power n: r → rn
• Multiply the angle by n: θ → nθ

That's it. No complicated algebra needed.

EXAMPLE: Calculate (1 + i)^8
SOLUTION

Step 1: Convert to polar form
z = 1 + i
r = √(1² + 1²) = √2
θ = arctan(1/1) = 45° = π/4
So: z = √2 · e^(iπ/4)

Step 2: Apply De Moivre
z^8 = (√2)^8 · e^(i·8·π/4)
= (2^(1/2))^8 · e^(i·2π)
= 2^4 · e^(i·2π)
= 16 · (cos 2π + i sin 2π)
= 16 · (1 + 0i)
= 16

Try doing (1+i)^8 by multiplying out algebraically. De Moivre is MUCH faster.

COMMON MISTAKES WITH DE MOIVRE

1. Forgetting to convert to polar first
De Moivre only works in polar form. Always find r and θ FIRST.

2. Not simplifying the angle
If nθ > 360°, subtract 360° until you get a standard angle.

3. Sign errors in the final step
After finding cos(nθ) and sin(nθ), be careful converting back to a + bi form.

7. Finding Roots of Complex Numbers

STANDARD PROCEDURE

To find all n-th roots of a complex number z:

Step 1: Convert z to polar form: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)
Step 2: Apply the formula for each k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1
Step 3: Convert each root back to rectangular form if needed

There are always EXACTLY n roots for an n-th root. No more, no less.

De Moivre works in reverse too. To find the n-th roots of a complex number:

n-TH ROOTS FORMULA
The n-th roots of z = r·e^(iθ) are: w_k = r^(1/n) · e^(i(θ + 2πk)/n) for k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1 There are exactly n roots, evenly spaced around a circle!
THE PATTERN

The n roots of any number are:
• All at the same distance from origin: r^(1/n)
• Evenly spaced: 360°/n apart
• First root at angle: θ/n

They form a regular n-gon on the complex plane!

EXAMPLE: Find all cube roots of 8
SOLUTION

z = 8 = 8·e^(i·0) (magnitude 8, angle 0)

The 3 cube roots:

k = 0: w₀ = 8^(1/3) · e^(i·0/3) = 2 · e^0 = 2

k = 1: w₁ = 2 · e^(i·2π/3) = 2(cos 120° + i sin 120°)
= 2(-1/2 + i·√3/2) = -1 + √3·i

k = 2: w₂ = 2 · e^(i·4π/3) = 2(cos 240° + i sin 240°)
= 2(-1/2 - i·√3/2) = -1 - √3·i

The 3 cube roots of 8 form an equilateral triangle: i ↑ | • w₁ = -1 + √3i | / | / | / ←———————————————+———•———————→ w₀ = 2 w₂ = -1 - √3i \ | \ ↓ All three roots have magnitude 2. They're spaced 120° apart (360°/3).
VISUALIZATION TIP

The roots form a regular polygon!

• 2 roots = 2 points on opposite sides (180° apart)
• 3 roots = equilateral triangle (120° apart)
• 4 roots = square (90° apart)
• 5 roots = pentagon (72° apart)
• n roots = regular n-gon (360°/n apart)

Sketch the circle, mark the first root at angle θ/n, then add points every 360°/n. This visual check helps catch errors!

8. Trig Identities — They Come From Geometry

THE STANDARD WAY — MEMORIZE THESE

On the AP exam, you're expected to know these:

Sum/Difference:
sin(A ± B) = sin A cos B ± cos A sin B
cos(A ± B) = cos A cos B ∓ sin A sin B

Double Angle:
sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ
cos(2θ) = cos²θ - sin²θ = 2cos²θ - 1 = 1 - 2sin²θ

Power Reduction:
sin²θ = (1 - cos 2θ)/2
cos²θ = (1 + cos 2θ)/2

Here's the payoff. All those trig identities you memorize? They're not arbitrary. They come directly from complex number geometry. Understanding where they come from makes them easier to remember.

Where Sum Formulas Come From

THE DERIVATION

e^(i(A+B)) = e^(iA) · e^(iB)

Left side: cos(A+B) + i sin(A+B)
Right side: (cos A + i sin A)(cos B + i sin B)

Expand the right side and match real/imaginary parts = sum formulas!

DERIVING THE SUM FORMULAS
e^(iA) · e^(iB) = (cos A + i sin A)(cos B + i sin B) Expand: = cos A cos B + i cos A sin B + i sin A cos B + i² sin A sin B = cos A cos B + i cos A sin B + i sin A cos B - sin A sin B = (cos A cos B - sin A sin B) + i(sin A cos B + cos A sin B) This must equal e^(i(A+B)) = cos(A+B) + i sin(A+B) Matching real parts: cos(A+B) = cos A cos B - sin A sin B Matching imaginary parts: sin(A+B) = sin A cos B + cos A sin B THAT'S where those formulas come from!

Where Double Angle Formulas Come From

DOUBLE ANGLE = SQUARING
e^(i·2θ) = (e^(iθ))² = (cos θ + i sin θ)² = cos²θ + 2i sin θ cos θ + i² sin²θ = (cos²θ - sin²θ) + i(2 sin θ cos θ) Therefore: cos(2θ) = cos²θ - sin²θ sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ The double angle formulas ARE the algebra of squaring e^(iθ).
THE INSIGHT

You don't have to memorize trig identities.

If you understand that e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ,
you can DERIVE any identity by doing algebra with exponentials.

Addition of angles = multiplication of e^(iθ)
Subtraction of angles = division of e^(iθ)
Double angles = squaring e^(iθ)
Half angles = square rooting e^(iθ)

Quick Reference: The Identities That Come From This

SUM & DIFFERENCE
sin(A ± B) = sin A cos B ± cos A sin B cos(A ± B) = cos A cos B ∓ sin A sin B (Note the ∓ — cosine flips the sign)
DOUBLE ANGLE
sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ cos(2θ) = cos²θ - sin²θ = 2cos²θ - 1 = 1 - 2sin²θ
POWER REDUCTION
sin²θ = (1 - cos 2θ)/2 cos²θ = (1 + cos 2θ)/2 (These come from solving the double angle formulas for sin² and cos²)

CALCULATOR EXAMPLE 3: Find the 4th roots of 16
TI CALCULATOR STEPS — FINDING ALL ROOTS

The calculator only gives ONE root. To find all 4, you need to do some work:

Step 1: Find the first root
Type: 16^(1/4) → shows 2
(This is root w₀ at angle 0°)

Step 2: Calculate remaining roots using the formula
The roots are spaced 360°/4 = 90° apart

w₁ at 90°: Type 2*e^(π/2*i) → shows 2i
w₂ at 180°: Type 2*e^(π*i) → shows -2

CALCULATOR GOTCHAS

1. Angle in wrong mode
If you're in DEGREE mode but expecting radians (or vice versa), your answers will be wrong. Always check MODE first!

2. Only one root shown
For n-th roots, the calculator only gives the principal root. You must calculate the others yourself using the 360°/n spacing.

3. Round-off errors
You might see answers like 1.999999999+0.0000001i instead of just 2. The calculator has tiny rounding errors. Recognize that this means 2.

THE TAKEAWAY

Complex numbers are not abstract weirdness.
They are points on a plane.

Multiplication is rotation + scaling.
e^(iθ) means "rotate by θ".
Trig identities are properties of rotation.

Once you see the geometry, the formulas stop being arbitrary.
