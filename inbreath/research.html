THE RESEARCH

Evidence for Inbreath as Universal Language

The Academic Foundation

Primary Source: Robert Eklund

The most comprehensive research on pulmonic ingressive speech comes from Robert Eklund, Professor of Phonetics at Linköping University, Sweden.

Key Publication

Eklund, R. (2008). "Pulmonic ingressive phonation: Diachronic and synchronic characteristics, distribution and function in animal and human sound production and in human speech."

Journal of the International Phonetic Association, 38(3), 235-324.

This 90-page paper documents:

  • ~50 languages with ingressive speech
  • Acoustic analysis and spectrograms
  • Evolutionary/biological basis
  • Historical documentation back to 1765

Key Findings

"Pulmonic ingressive phonation is common in the calls of monkeys and apes... it is suggested that ingressive speech might constitute a neglected universal phenomenon."

"In normal spoken conversation, present-day ingressive speech is not limited to Scandinavia or Nordic languages, but is found on all continents, in genetically unrelated languages."

"Where ingressive speech occurs, it serves more or less the same paralinguistic functions, such as a feedback marker in a dialog."

Historical Documentation

The Oldest Written Record

Cranz, David. (1765). Historie von Grönland.

German Moravian missionary David Cranz documented ingressive affirmatives among Greenlandic Inuit women — the first known written record of this phenomenon.

This predates modern linguistics by over a century.

The Biological Evidence

Primate Research

Peters, Gustav. (2002). "Purring and similar vocalizations in mammals." Mammal Review, 3(4), 245-271.

Documents ingressive phonation in mammalian vocalizations, supporting the evolutionary basis for human ingressive speech.

Peters, G. & Tonkin-Leyhausen, B.A. (1999). "Evolution of Acoustic Communication Signals of Mammals." Journal of Mammalian Evolution, 6(2), 129-159.

Shows the biological continuity of ingressive vocalization from primates to humans.

The Damin Case

The Only Phonemic Pulmonic Ingressive

Hale, Kenneth. (1973). "Deep-Surface Canonical Disparities in Relation to Analysis and Change." Current Trends in Linguistics 11, 401-458.

Primary documentation of Damin, including its unique pulmonic ingressive phoneme.

What Made Damin Unique

Damin was:

  • A ceremonial register (not everyday language)
  • Used only by initiated men
  • The only language outside Africa with click consonants
  • The only language with a phonemic pulmonic ingressive [ɬ↓ʔ]
  • Extinct since the 1990s

The Disputed Case: Tsou

Fuller, Michael. (1990). "Pulmonic ingressive fricatives in Tsou." Journal of the International Phonetic Association 20(2), 9-14.

Claimed pulmonic ingressive fricatives in Tsou (Taiwan).

Ladefoged, Peter & Zeitoun, Elizabeth. (1993). "Pulmonic ingressive phones do not occur in Tsou." Journal of the International Phonetic Association 23(1), 13-15.

Refuted Fuller's findings — could not replicate.

Current Consensus

No confirmed pulmonic ingressive phonemes exist in any living language. Damin was the only confirmed case, and it is extinct.

Conclusion

The academic evidence supports treating pulmonic ingressive speech as:

  • A human universal (or near-universal)
  • Biologically inherited rather than culturally transmitted
  • Functionally consistent across all documented languages
  • A navigation system for dialogue and attention

The inbreath language is not new. It is ancient.
The research is not speculative. It is documented.
The phenomenon is not rare. It is ubiquitous.

We simply haven't been paying attention.
