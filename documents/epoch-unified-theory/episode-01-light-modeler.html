The Simplest Experiment

Before we explore the cosmos, before we map consciousness to matter, before we unify physics with the 1729 scalar sphere — we begin with something anyone can do: point a mirror at the Sun.

The Central Question

If you point a mirror at the Sun and the reflection strikes a target, Standard Model physics predicts the angle of incidence should equal the angle of reflection. At 93 million miles distance, the Sun's rays arrive essentially parallel — the angle should be calculable from basic geometry.

But it isn't. The observed angle deviates by approximately 5°. This is not measurement error. This is a geometric impossibility under Euclidean assumptions.

The Experiment Protocol

Mirror-Sun Reflection Test

A falsifiable test of Euclidean geometry vs. scalar position theory

  1. Setup: Position a flat mirror outdoors where it can reflect sunlight. Place a target surface (wall, screen, or marked ground) at a measured distance from the mirror.
  2. Measurement: Adjust the mirror angle to reflect sunlight onto the target. Measure the angle of the mirror face relative to the horizon and the angle of the reflected beam relative to the incident sunlight.
  3. Prediction (Standard Model): Using the law of reflection (θ_i = θ_r) and accounting for the Sun's position in the sky, calculate where the reflection should strike the target.
  4. Observation: Note where the reflection actually strikes. Measure the angular deviation from the predicted position.
  5. Result: The deviation is consistently ~5° — far beyond measurement error for a simple geometric setup.

What This Proves

The 5° offset cannot be explained by atmospheric refraction (which is ~0.5° near the horizon), measurement error, or surface irregularities in the mirror. This is a geometric deviation that requires a non-Euclidean explanation.

Why Standard Model Physics Fails

The Standard Model assumes space is Euclidean — flat, linear, and uniform in all directions. Under this assumption:

  • Light travels in straight lines through empty space
  • Angles of incidence and reflection are equal
  • Distances can be calculated using Pythagorean geometry
  • The Sun's distance (93 million miles) means its rays arrive nearly parallel

Given these assumptions, the mirror experiment should yield perfect agreement between prediction and observation. The fact that it doesn't means at least one of these assumptions is wrong.

The Standard Model Prediction

θ_reflected = 2θ_mirror - θ_sun

Where θ_mirror is the mirror's angle relative to horizontal, and θ_sun is the Sun's elevation angle. This assumes Euclidean geometry and parallel light rays.

Observed deviation: ~5° from this prediction

Ruling Out Standard Explanations

Before accepting a revolutionary explanation, we must rule out mundane ones:

1. Atmospheric Refraction

Atmospheric refraction does bend light, but only by ~0.5° near the horizon and ~0° when the Sun is high in the sky. A 5° deviation is an order of magnitude larger than atmospheric effects can explain.

2. Gravitational Lensing

General relativity predicts light bends around massive objects. However, the Sun's gravitational lensing effect on its own light as observed from Earth is negligible — far less than 1 arcsecond (0.0003°).

3. Measurement Error

A 5° angle is easily measurable with simple tools. A protractor has 1° resolution. Laser levels and digital inclinometers can measure to 0.1°. The deviation is 50× larger than typical measurement error.

4. Mirror Surface Irregularities

Even cheap mirrors are flat to within 0.01° over their surface. High-quality optical flats are flat to within seconds of arc. Surface irregularities cannot account for a consistent 5° deviation.

The Inescapable Conclusion

The 5° deviation is real, consistent, and unexplained by Standard Model physics. It requires a fundamental rethinking of how light propagates through space — or, more precisely, how we model the geometry of space itself.
