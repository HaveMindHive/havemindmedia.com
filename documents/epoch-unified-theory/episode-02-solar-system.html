EPISODE 02 • ORBITAL MECHANICS

Solar System Geometry

Ellipses, barycenters, and the wobble that reveals triaxial gravitational systems

The Question No One Asks

Why do planets orbit the Sun in ellipses rather than circles?

Standard physics has an answer: Kepler's laws, derived from Newton's law of gravitation. Planets follow elliptical orbits because gravity creates a central force, and the solutions to the equations of motion are conic sections — circles, ellipses, parabolas, or hyperbolas, depending on the energy and angular momentum of the system.

This is mathematically correct. But it doesn't answer the why.

Why is gravity a central force? Why do conic sections arise? Why does the solar system settle into this particular geometric configuration rather than another?

The Epoch Framework offers a different answer: Elliptical orbits are the signature of triaxial balance in a scalar field.

The Standard Model: Two-Body Problem

In Newtonian mechanics, planetary motion is analyzed as a two-body problem: the Sun and one planet, mutually attracting each other through gravity.

Newton's Law of Gravitation

F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r²

Where G is the gravitational constant, m₁ and m₂ are the masses of the two bodies, and r is the distance between their centers.

This creates an inverse-square force — the force drops off with the square of the distance.

Solving the equations of motion for this force yields Kepler's three laws:

  1. First Law (Law of Ellipses): Planets orbit the Sun in elliptical paths, with the Sun at one focus of the ellipse.
  2. Second Law (Law of Equal Areas): A line connecting the planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times.
  3. Third Law (Law of Periods): The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its semi-major axis (P² ∝ a³).

These laws are descriptive — they tell us how planets move. But they don't explain why this geometry emerges from the underlying structure of space.

The Circular Orbit "Special Case"

In the Standard Model, a circular orbit is a special case of an ellipse where the eccentricity (e) equals zero. Most planets have elliptical orbits with small but nonzero eccentricities:

Planet Orbital Eccentricity (e) Interpretation
Mercury 0.206 Highly elliptical
Venus 0.007 Nearly circular
Earth 0.017 Slightly elliptical
Mars 0.093 Noticeably elliptical
Jupiter 0.049 Moderately elliptical
Saturn 0.056 Moderately elliptical

The Standard Model treats the eccentricity as an initial condition — a result of how the solar system formed. If planets had formed with exactly the right velocity at exactly the right distance, they would orbit in perfect circles. But they didn't, so they don't.

This is unsatisfying. It leaves the geometry of the solar system to chance, rather than deriving it from fundamental principles.

The Ellipse as Torsional Signature

An ellipse has two foci rather than one center. In the Standard Model, this is explained geometrically: an ellipse is the set of all points where the sum of distances to the two foci is constant.

In the Epoch Framework, the two foci represent two torsional poles:

  • Focus 1: The Sun's physical position (where mass concentrates)

The planet orbits both foci — not just the Sun. The empty focus is not "nothing" — it's the scalar position that balances the Sun's mass against the triaxial torsional forces acting on the planet.

Where:

The Barycenter: The System Wobbles

The Standard Model acknowledges that the Sun doesn't sit motionless at the center of the solar system. Instead, the Sun and planets orbit their common center of mass — the barycenter.

What Is the Barycenter?

The barycenter is the point where the total mass of the system balances. For two bodies of unequal mass, the barycenter lies closer to the more massive object.

This means the Sun wobbles as Jupiter orbits. The Sun is not stationary — it moves in a small ellipse around the barycenter.

The Multi-Body Wobble

It's not just Jupiter. Every planet in the solar system contributes to the barycentric wobble. Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune also pull the Sun around. The result is a complex, multi-axis wobble — the Sun traces a chaotic path through space as it responds to the combined gravitational pulls of all the planets.

Observed Solar Wobble

Astronomers can measure the Sun's wobble by observing its motion relative to the background stars (via stellar parallax or Doppler shift). The Sun's velocity varies by approximately:

  • ±13 m/s due to Jupiter's orbit (11.86-year period)
  • ±3 m/s due to Saturn's orbit (29.46-year period)
  • Smaller contributions from Uranus, Neptune, and the inner planets

This wobble is how we detect exoplanets around other stars — by measuring the star's wobble, we infer the presence of orbiting planets.

What the Wobble Reveals

In the Standard Model, the barycentric wobble is a consequence of Newton's third law: every action has an equal and opposite reaction. The Sun pulls on the planets, so the planets pull back on the Sun.

In the Epoch Framework, the wobble reveals something deeper: the solar system is not a stable, static structure. It is a dynamic balance state — a triaxial torsional system constantly adjusting to maintain equilibrium.

The Wobble Is the Balance

The Sun's wobble is not a "perturbation" or "instability." It's the mechanism of balance itself. The Sun moves because it must move to keep the scalar field in equilibrium.

If the Sun were perfectly stationary, the system would collapse. The wobble is the stability.
