Personal Note: The Seeds of Wonder

On Carl Sagan's "Cosmos"

I never missed an episode.

I would record them on VHS tapes—those chunky black cassettes that could hold the universe if you were careful with the tracking—and watch them over and over again. The opening music. The dandelion seed floating through space. Carl's voice, gentle and certain and filled with something I didn't have words for then but now know to call reverent curiosity.

"The cosmos is all that is, or ever was, or ever will be."

Those words imprinted on me. Not as dogma but as invitation. Here was a man who could speak about billions of years and make you feel them. Who could describe the death of stars and make you grateful to be made of their debris.

Cosmos didn't just teach me science. It taught me that asking questions is itself a form of worship—the only honest kind. It taught me that wonder and rigor are not opposites but partners. It taught me that a human being can stand on a small blue dot and comprehend, even if only partially, the shape of everything.

Every equation in this document, every alloy composition, every mirror specification—they all trace back, in some way, to those nights sitting too close to the television, rewinding the tape to hear Carl explain the library of Alexandria one more time.

This Star Gate is, in the end, a very elaborate thank you note to a man who showed a kid from Texas that the universe was not only knowable but worth knowing.

For Carl.
For the tapes.
For the wonder that never stopped.
